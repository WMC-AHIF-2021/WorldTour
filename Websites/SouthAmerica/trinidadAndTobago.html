  • Black Cake


    Black cake is the biggest Christmas tradition in Trinidad and Tobago. Caribbean people always say that without it, Christmas would not be complete.
    It is considered that this cake undoubtedly evolved from plum pudding recipes brought to the West Indies region by English settlers in the 18th century. The natives modified these dishes by incorporating not only local ingredients but also different types of liquors. It is common in these countries for black cake to be served along with other dishes such as roast ham, sorrel (spiced hibiscus tea) and rice.

  • Culture - Trinidad & Tobago


    Depending upon which island in this twin–island state is being discussed, the culture name is "Trinidadian" or "Tobagonian."

    The country is noted for its religiosity and religious diversity.

    Official religious holidays:
    - Divali (called Holi in India; Hindu),
    - Eid (Muslim),
    - Spiritual Baptist Liberation Day (30 March),
    - Good Friday,
    - Corpus Christi,
    - and Christmas Day.

  • Special Sights


    Naturally Tobago is Trinidad's less-developed "younger sister". Rainforests, reefs, and white-sand beaches are the prime tourist attractions here, with excellent opportunities for snorkeling and diving.
    The islands attract many independent travelers who appreciate the unpretentious ambience and dramatic topography.
    In particular, both islands are renowned for their excellent birding, with many avian species from nearby South America enriching the biodiversity.
    Trinidad and Tobago is also famous for its Carnival, held on the Monday and Tuesday before Ash Wednesday.
